Today I will be expanding on that technique, so you can start to make your own scalable UI elements for WebKit.</p><p style="margin-top: 0px; margin-right: 0px; margin-bottom: 12px; margin-left: 0px; padding-top: 0px; padding-right: 0px; padding-bottom: 0px; padding-left: 0px; color: #333333;"><img id="photocomparison" src="http://www.sencha.com/blog/wp-content/uploads/2010/08/resolution-independent-ui/resources/button_photo.jpg" alt="Photo comparison of a high pixel density device, an iPhone 4, and a less dense device, the iPad." width="675" style="border-top-width: 0px; border-right-width: 0px; border-bottom-width: 0px; border-left-width: 0px; border-style: initial; border-color: initial;" /></p><h3 id="abriefintroduction" style="margin-top: 1.2em; margin-right: 0px; margin-bottom: 2px; margin-left: 0px; padding-top: 0px; padding-right: 0px; padding-bottom: 0px; padding-left: 0px; font-size: 16px; font-weight: 600; font-family: 'Helvetica Neue', Helvetica, Arial, sans-serif; text-rendering: optimizelegibility; color: #444444; letter-spacing: normal; line-height: 18px;">A Brief Introduction</h3><p style="margin-top: 0px; margin-right: 0px; margin-bottom: 12px; margin-left: 0px; padding-top: 0px; padding-right: 0px; padding-bottom: 0px; padding-left: 0px; color: #333333;">This is not an article on adaptive layouts or responsive web design. This article is specifically about pixel “density”, a relatively new concern to digital designers. In the mobile environment, screen resolution has been quickly advancing, resulting in larger and larger “pixels per inch” (ppi).</p><p style="margin-top: 0px; margin-right: 0px; margin-bottom: 12px; margin-left: 0px; padding-top: 0px; padding-right: 0px; padding-bottom: 0px; padding-left: 0px; color: #333333;">Take, for example, the image above. This is the same page, rendered by the same browser, on the iPad and iPhone 4. Note how the button looks smaller on the iPhone. This is because the iPad has a pixel density of 132ppi, while the iPhone 4, with its Retina Display, has a ppi of 326. The iPhone 4, however, automatically scales pages 2×, so we are effectively working with a ppi of 163. This is still about 15% denser than the iPad display, which means the&nbsp;<em style="font-style: italic; font-weight: normal;">physical</em>&nbsp;display of the button button is about 15% smaller.</p><p style="margin-top: 0px; margin-right: 0px; margin-bottom: 12px; margin-left: 0px; padding-top: 0px; padding-right: 0px; padding-bottom: 0px; padding-left: 0px; color: #333333;">This pixel density must be taken into account when designing cross-platform mobile applications, for two primary reasons:</p><ul style="margin-top: 10px; margin-right: 16px; margin-bottom: 10px; margin-left: 16px; padding-top: 0px; padding-right: 0px; padding-bottom: 0px; padding-left: 0px; list-style-type: square; list-style-position: initial; list-style-image: url(http://www.sencha.com/assets/images/arrow-blue.gif);"><li style="margin-top: 2px; margin-right: 10px; margin-bottom: 2px; margin-left: 0px; padding-top: 0px; padding-right: 0px; padding-bottom: 0px; padding-left: 0px; line-height: 1.5em;">If you design a button for a 132ppi (like the iPad), it will come out much smaller for higher ppis, which could result in it becoming harder to tap and possibly render it unusable.</li><li style="margin-top: 2px; margin-right: 10px; margin-bottom: 2px; margin-left: 0px; padding-top: 0px; padding-right: 0px; padding-bottom: 0px; padding-left: 0px; line-height: 1.5em;">Images and UI elements must be scaled intelligently, to avoid pixellation and blurriness.</li></ul><h3 id="thegeneralconcept" style="margin-top: 1.2em; margin-right: 0px; margin-bottom: 2px; margin-left: 0px; padding-top: 0px; padding-right: 0px; padding-bottom: 0px; padding-left: 0px; font-size: 16px; font-weight: 600; font-family: 'Helvetica Neue', Helvetica, Arial, sans-serif; text-rendering: optimizelegibility; color: #444444; letter-spacing: normal; line-height: 18px;">The General Concept</h3><p style="margin-top: 0px; margin-right: 0px; margin-bottom: 12px; margin-left: 0px; padding-top: 0px; padding-right: 0px; padding-bottom: 0px; padding-left: 0px; color: #333333;">The overriding concept behind this technique is to use ems — a typographically relative measurement unit — to size your elements. Wikipedia describes an Em as such:</p><blockquote style="margin-top: 0px; margin-right: 0px; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-left: 0px; padding-top: 0px; padding-right: 0px; padding-bottom: 0px; padding-left: 0px;"><p style="margin-top: 0px; margin-right: 0px; margin-bottom: 12px; margin-left: 0px; padding-top: 0px; padding-right: 0px; padding-bottom: 0px; padding-left: 0px; color: #333333;">This unit defines the proportion of the letter width and height with respect to the point size of the current font… This unit is not defined in terms of any specific typeface, and thus is the same for all fonts at a given point size. So, 1 em in a 16 point typeface is 16 points.</p></blockquote><p style="margin-top: 0px; margin-right: 0px; margin-bottom: 12px; margin-left: 0px; padding-top: 0px; padding-right: 0px; padding-bottom: 0px; padding-left: 0px; color: #333333;">Because&nbsp;<code style="font-style: normal; font-weight: normal;">font-size</code>&nbsp;is an inherited property in CSS, we are able to change the scale of any&nbsp;<code style="font-style: normal;
